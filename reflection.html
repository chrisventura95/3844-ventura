Reflection

Based on Module 1’s assigned readings, I was surprised at how much thought was put into the idea of an all-accessible encyclopedia. Both H.G. Wells in “World Brain” and Vannevar Bush in “As We May Think” accurately predicted the need for transferrable knowledge, and they also predicted technology that wouldn’t exist for another 60 years. Until this semester, I had no idea that scholars valued the role of data archival and data distribution in our society.

I was intrigued reading articles about the College of Liberal Arts and Human Sciences utilizing a “VT-shaped student” concept. While I love double majoring in English and Communication, it’s a little overwhelming being around engineers and computer science majors. After reading articles such as “Concepts” from the Beyond Boundaries website, I was amazed at how much was planned for the “VT-shaped student” concept. The article contains huge expectations for students to engage with the community and make big-scale, environmentally friendly, cost-effective ideas that positively impact the world. Learning opportunities outside of the classroom are emphasized so students gain hands-on experience as they promote the Virginia Tech name wherever they go.

I felt proud knowing that the liberal arts department focuses their attention on the students’ versatility and ensures that the students acquire a diverse skillset. I’m glad we’re learning how to apply our writing skills to the technology field.

At first, building HTML websites was difficult to get used to. It’s repetitive having to open and close brackets after every heading, paragraph, list, etc. Sometimes, I forget all together, and it messes up the entire code. The process of adding a picture and making sure it sizes correctly on the website takes a lot longer than I thought it would. Now that I am completing assignments for Writing and Digital Media, I understand the meticulous steps that are necessary in order to build websites that have concise content and look visually appealing.

The feeling after writing long lines of code and checking the website, only to find that the code didn’t work at all, is truly awful. It means I have to go back and look through each line of code again and again to spot the mistake(s). Sometimes, I have to go look up the problem on Google and see if other users have found any solutions. While the process is long, it is ultimately rewarding. Not only am I learning essential coding skills, I am also creating content that I am proud to say is my own. It’s a big step up from Weebly, where I’m simply dragging and dropping text boxes and images. Instead, I am building content from the ground up.

All in all, my experience with Module 1 was uncomfortable and repetitious; however, I can honestly say I learned more in these few weeks than I have during the whole semester in some of my other classes. Looking back on how much I have experienced, I cannot wait to see what is in store from future modules and what else I can learn about digital writing.
